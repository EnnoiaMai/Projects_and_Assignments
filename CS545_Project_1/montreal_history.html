History of Montreal

Jacques Cartier was the first European to make land on what is now known in modern day as Montreal in 1535. The area was inhabited by the Iroquois. Thereafter in 1609, Samuel de Champlain attempted to create a trading post for fur in the area, which became a part of New France since the country had goals to expand. There were missionaries who sailed with him and including Paul de Chomedey, who founded and established the colony named "Ville-Marie de Montreal". This location which intersects both the St Lawrence and Ottawa rivers was ideal for trade and communication. Although the French struggled against the Iroquois, it wasn't until 1760 that the French had to give in to the British after losing the war.

By the late 1800s, Montreal prospered commercially with the fur trade being avenues for merchants. A trade system that took advantage of Montreal's ports further contributed to the growth of the city. For a short time, Montreal was the capital of of the United Canadas between 1844 and 1849.

From 1929 to 1939, the Great Depression reached Canada leading to losses in companies and prices and increasing unemployment. In 1944, the Conscription Crisis occurred leading to protests against enforced enlistment during World War II.

Montreal's status as a cultural hub was even more convincing after the events of Expo 67 which was a general exhibition or a World Fair hosted by the city. The 1976 Summer Olympics was also hosted in Montreal which solidified the city's cultural impression. What is notable was the merging and blending of French and English cultures due to historical developments which facilitated the emergence of this diverse and unique cultures in terms of the food, the interactions, and the atmosphere of Montreal.
